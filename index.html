Recent JWST observations showing a 65-35% galaxy spin asymmetry in deep fields provide compelling preliminary evidence for cosmic parity violation, consistent with our rotating universe model predictions.

1. Introduction

The nature of dark energy remains one of the most significant challenges in modern cosmology. While the $\Lambda$CDM model successfully describes many observations, it faces theoretical difficulties including the cosmological constant problem and coincidence problem, alongside growing observational tensions. Recent measurements reveal a $4.4\sigma$ discrepancy in the Hubble constant between early-universe (Planck: $H_0 = 67.36 \pm 0.54$ km s$^{-1}$ Mpc$^{-1}$) and late-universe (SH0ES: $H_0 = 73.04 \pm 1.04$ km s$^{-1}$ Mpc$^{-1}$) determinations, alongside a $2.5\sigma$ tension in the matter clustering amplitude $\sigma_8$ between CMB and weak lensing measurements.

This work presents a theoretical framework that potentially addresses these challenges through quantum gravitational effects in spin-torsion cosmology. Our approach builds on three key theoretical pillars: (1) Loop Quantum Cosmology (LQC) providing a non-singular quantum bounce, (2) Einstein-Cartan theory incorporating fermionic spin-torsion coupling, and (3) false-vacuum bubble nucleation driving slow-roll inflation within a rotating black hole interior. The integration of these elements yields a parity-odd effective action that naturally generates dark energy through geometric dilution, while making distinctive observational predictions testable within the current decade.

Appendix A: 1+3 Covariant Derivation of Rotation Term

A.1 Setup and Notation

We work in the 1+3 covariant formalism with a timelike congruence $u^a$ (normalized $u^a u_a = -1$). The covariant derivative of the 4-velocity is decomposed as:

$$\nabla_b u_a = -u_b \dot{u}_a + \frac{1}{3}\Theta h_{ab} + \sigma_{ab} + \omega_{ab}$$

where:

  • $\dot{u}_a = u^b\nabla_b u_a$ is the 4-acceleration
  • $\Theta = \nabla_a u^a = 3H$ is the expansion scalar
  • $\sigma_{ab}$ is the shear tensor (symmetric, traceless, orthogonal to $u^a$)
  • $\omega_{ab}$ is the vorticity tensor (antisymmetric, orthogonal to $u^a$)
  • $h_{ab} = g_{ab} + u_a u_b$ is the projection tensor

The magnitudes are defined as:

$$\sigma^2 \equiv \frac{1}{2}\sigma_{ab}\sigma^{ab}, \quad \omega^2 \equiv \frac{1}{2}\omega_{ab}\omega^{ab}$$

A.2 The Constraint Equation

The Gauss-Codazzi equations relate the 3-Ricci scalar ${}^3R$ of spatial hypersurfaces to the 4-dimensional quantities. After a lengthy but standard calculation (see Ellis & van Elst 1999), one obtains:

$$\frac{2}{3}\Theta^2 = 8\pi G\rho + \Lambda - \frac{1}{2}{}^3R + \sigma^2 - \omega^2$$

For an almost-FLRW universe with small shear and vorticity, we can write ${}^3R = 6k/a^2$ where $k$ is the spatial curvature constant. With $\Theta = 3H$, this becomes:

$$H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho + \frac{\Lambda}{3} - \frac{k}{a^2} + \frac{1}{3}(\sigma^2 - \omega^2)$$

A.3 Effective Cosmological Constant

Comparing with the standard Friedmann equation, we can absorb the shear and vorticity contributions into an effective cosmological constant:

$$\Lambda_{\rm eff} = \Lambda + (\sigma^2 - \omega^2)$$

Note the crucial signs:

  • Shear increases the expansion rate (positive contribution)
  • Vorticity decreases the expansion rate (negative contribution)

In the limit of negligible shear ($\sigma^2 \ll \omega^2$), we have:

$$\Lambda_{\rm eff} = \Lambda - \omega^2$$

Therefore, $c_\omega = -1$ in our notation. Different conventions for the normalization of $\omega_{ab}$ can lead to factors of 2 or 3, which is why we carry $c_\omega$ symbolically in the main text.

A.4 The Raychaudhuri Equation

For completeness, the evolution of expansion is governed by:

$$\dot{\Theta} + \frac{1}{3}\Theta^2 + 2(\sigma^2 - \omega^2) + 4\pi G(\rho + 3p) - \Lambda = 0$$

Again, vorticity appears with opposite sign to shear, providing a "centrifugal" resistance to gravitational focusing.

A.5 Isotropy Constraints

Current observational bounds from CMB isotropy studies (Planck 2018) give:

$$\left(\frac{\omega}{H}\right)_0 < 5 \times 10^{-11} \quad \text{(95% CL)}$$

This implies:

$$\frac{|\omega^2|}{H_0^2} < 2.5 \times 10^{-21}$$

For any reasonable $c_\omega \sim \mathcal{O}(1)$, the rotation contribution to the effective cosmological constant is:

$$\frac{|c_\omega \omega^2|}{3H_0^2} < 10^{-21} \times \Omega_{\Lambda,0} \sim 10^{-21}$$

This is completely negligible for background cosmology. Rotation manifests only through parity-violating observables that probe the direction of $\omega^a$, not its magnitude squared.

Appendix B: Loop Corrections and Nieh-Yan Treatment

B.1 From Einstein-Cartan-Holst to Axial-Axial Contact Term

Starting with the Einstein-Cartan-Holst action with real Barbero-Immirzi parameter $\gamma$:

$$S[e,\omega,\psi] = \frac{1}{16\pi G} \int d^4x \, e \left[R + \frac{1}{\gamma} \varepsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\right] + S_{\psi}[e,\omega,\psi]$$

where $e = \det(e_\mu^a)$ is the tetrad determinant. When fermions are present, varying with respect to the spin connection $\omega$ yields an algebraic constraint relating torsion to the axial current:

$$T^a_{\mu\nu} = \kappa \varepsilon^a_{bcd} e^b_\mu e^c_\nu J^d_5$$

where $J^a_5 = \bar{\psi} \gamma^a \gamma^5 \psi$ is the axial current and $\kappa = 8\pi G$.

Eliminating torsion by substituting back into the action yields the well-known axial-axial four-fermion contact term:

$$\mathcal{L}_{AA} = -\frac{3\pi G}{2} \cdot \frac{\gamma^2}{\gamma^2+1} \cdot (J^a_5 J_{5a})$$

This is parity-even and represents the leading-order effect of torsion on fermion dynamics.

B.2 Parity-Odd Term and Nieh-Yan Regularization

At one loop, quantum corrections generate a parity-odd operator. The key subtlety involves the relationship between the Holst density and the Nieh-Yan 4-form:

$$\mathcal{NY} = d(e^a \wedge T_a) - e^a \wedge e^b \wedge R_{ab}$$

In the torsion-free sector, these differ by a total derivative. However, with dynamical torsion from fermions, they are inequivalent.

B.3 Regularization Scheme and Finite Coefficient

We adopt dimensional regularization to handle the one-loop divergences. The parity-odd effective operator emerges as:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\rm odd} = \frac{\alpha}{M} \varepsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} K_{\mu\nu}{}^{ab} R_{\rho\sigma ab}$$

where $K$ is the contorsion tensor. The coefficient has the structure:

$$\frac{\alpha}{M} = \frac{g^2}{32\pi^2} \cdot \frac{\gamma}{M} \cdot \ln\left(\frac{\Lambda^2}{\mu^2}\right) + \delta_{\rm NY}(\mu)$$

Key points:

  1. $g$ is the axial-torsion coupling fixed by Einstein-Cartan geometry
  2. $\Lambda$ is a UV cutoff (e.g., LQG area-gap scale)
  3. $\mu$ is the renormalization scale
  4. $\delta_{\rm NY}(\mu)$ is the finite Nieh-Yan counterterm

B.4 Renormalization Condition

We fix the renormalized coefficient by requiring that the observed dark energy scale emerges after inflationary dilution:

$$\left(\frac{\alpha}{M}\right)_{\rm ren} \times \mathcal{D}_{\rm inf} = \Lambda_{\rm obs} \approx (2.3 \text{ meV})^4$$

This provides a definite prediction for the parity-odd coupling at any scale $\mu$.

Appendix C: Hierarchical Bayesian Analysis of Galaxy Spin Asymmetry

C.1 Parametric Model

We model the galaxy spin asymmetry as a function of redshift:

$$A(z) = A_0 \cdot (1+z)^{-p} \cdot e^{-qz}$$

with parameters:

  • $A_0$: Present-day amplitude
  • $p$: Power-law index
  • $q$: Exponential decay rate

C.2 Hierarchical Structure

For survey $s$ with galaxies indexed by $i$:

Galaxy level:

$$\text{spin}_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\theta_i)$$

$$\text{logit}(\theta_i) = \alpha_s + A(z_i) \cos(\psi_i)$$

where $\psi_i$ is the angle from the dipole axis.

Survey level:

$$\alpha_s \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\alpha)$$

Hyperpriors:

  • $A_0 \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 0.02)$
  • $p \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 2)$
  • $q \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 2)$
  • $\sigma_\alpha \sim \text{HalfCauchy}(0, 0.1)$

C.3 Systematic Corrections

We include:

  1. PSF ellipticity bias: $\delta A_{\rm PSF} = f_{\rm PSF}(e, \theta)$
  2. Selection function: $w_i = S(m_i, r_i, z_i)$
  3. Classifier uncertainty: $P(\text{true spin} | \text{observed})$
